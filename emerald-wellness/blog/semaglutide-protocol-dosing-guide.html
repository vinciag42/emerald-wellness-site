What is Semaglutide and How Does It Work?

Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist — a synthetic analogue of glucagon-like peptide-1, an endogenous incretin hormone produced in the gut after eating. It was originally developed by Novo Nordisk for type 2 diabetes management and has since become one of the most prescribed and most studied drugs in the world following its approval for chronic weight management.

Its mechanisms are multifactorial and extend far beyond simple appetite suppression:

  • Central appetite suppression — activates GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus and brainstem, reducing hunger signals and food reward responses. Users describe a fundamental change in their relationship with food — cravings diminish significantly.
  • Gastric emptying delay — slows the rate at which the stomach empties into the small intestine, prolonging satiety and blunting postprandial glucose spikes
  • Insulin secretion enhancement — glucose-dependently increases insulin release from pancreatic beta cells (only when blood glucose is elevated — no hypoglycemia risk in non-diabetics)
  • Glucagon suppression — reduces hepatic glucose output by suppressing glucagon, improving overall glycemic control
  • Cardiovascular protection — the SUSTAIN and SELECT trials demonstrated significant reduction in MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events) — a benefit now considered independent of weight loss
  • Neuroprotection (emerging) — GLP-1 receptors are present throughout the brain; emerging research suggests semaglutide may reduce neuroinflammation and Alzheimer's risk

The Titration Schedule

Slow titration is the most important variable in semaglutide tolerability. The majority of people who discontinue semaglutide do so because of nausea — and the majority of those cases involve going too fast. The standard titration protocol:

1
Weeks 1–4
0.25 mg/week
Starting dose. Do not attempt weight loss at this dose — it's purely for GI adaptation. Inject once weekly, same day each week.
2
Weeks 5–8
0.5 mg/week
First therapeutic dose for most users. Appetite suppression becomes noticeable. If GI side effects are significant, stay at 0.25mg for an additional 4 weeks.
3
Weeks 9–12
1.0 mg/week
Sweet spot for most users — strong appetite suppression with manageable side effects. Many users achieve their goals without escalating further.
4
Weeks 13–16
1.7 mg/week
Escalate only if response at 1.0mg has plateaued. GI side effects increase at this step. Aggressive anti-nausea protocol required.
5
Weeks 17+
2.4 mg/week
Maximum approved dose (Wegovy). Reserve for patients who have not achieved adequate response at 1.7mg. Only ~40% of users need this dose for maximum effect.
💡 The Slow Titration Rule

If you experience significant nausea at any dose, do not advance to the next step. Stay at the current dose for an additional 4 weeks. GI adaptation continues with time — most people find their nausea resolves or becomes manageable within 2–4 weeks of any given dose.

Side Effects & How to Manage Every One

The Muscle Loss Problem — and How to Solve It

The most significant underreported concern with semaglutide is muscle mass loss. DEXA scan studies show that approximately 25–40% of weight lost on GLP-1 agonists is lean mass — not fat — in users who don't take specific countermeasures. This is not unique to semaglutide; it's a consequence of significant caloric restriction without adequate protein and resistance training stimulus.

This matters enormously for long-term health: muscle mass is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality and healthspan. Losing substantial lean mass while losing fat undermines the longevity benefits of weight loss.

✦ The Muscle Preservation Protocol
  • Protein: minimum 1.6g/kg bodyweight per day — prioritize protein at every meal, even when appetite is suppressed. Liquid protein (shakes) may be necessary on high-nausea days.
  • Resistance training: 3–4x per week — heavy compound movements (squat, deadlift, press, row). Progressive overload. This is non-negotiable.
  • Creatine monohydrate: 5g/day — prevents muscle loss during caloric restriction via multiple mechanisms; one of the most evidence-supported supplements for this specific context.
  • HMB (β-Hydroxy β-Methylbutyrate): 3g/day — leucine metabolite with documented anti-catabolic effects; particularly effective during caloric restriction.
  • Leucine: 2–3g with each protein meal — the primary amino acid trigger for muscle protein synthesis; ensure each meal contains enough to activate MPS (~2.5g leucine threshold).

⚡ Key Drug Interactions
  • Oral medications: Semaglutide delays gastric emptying — all oral medications should be taken 1 hour before or 3+ hours after injection to ensure proper absorption
  • Insulin and sulfonylureas: Hypoglycemia risk if combined — dose adjustment required under physician guidance
  • Warfarin: Monitor INR closely — GI changes can affect absorption of warfarin and alter anticoagulation
  • Thyroid medication (levothyroxine): Must be taken fasted, 30–60 min before any food or other medications — semaglutide delays absorption; take levothyroxine first thing in the morning on a separate schedule

🔄 Weight Regain Warning

The majority of users regain weight after discontinuing semaglutide without addressing the underlying behavioral and metabolic factors. Semaglutide works best as a tool to enable habit change, not as a permanent pharmacological fix. Use the appetite suppression window to build protein-first eating habits, establish consistent resistance training, and address sleep and stress — so the metabolic improvements persist after discontinuation.

FAQ

Do I need a prescription for semaglutide?

Yes — in the US, UK, EU, and most countries, semaglutide requires a prescription. Branded Ozempic and Wegovy require physician prescription. Compounded semaglutide from licensed pharmacies also technically requires a prescription in most jurisdictions. Telehealth platforms have made access significantly easier — a physician consultation is still required.

What's the difference between semaglutide and tirzepatide?

Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) is a dual GLP-1 and GIP agonist — it activates two incretin receptors instead of one. Clinical trials show tirzepatide produces greater average weight loss than semaglutide (20–22% body weight vs 15–17%). However, semaglutide has a longer safety and efficacy track record. Both are strong choices; tirzepatide may be preferred for maximum weight loss, semaglutide for its broader cardiovascular evidence base.

How do I inject semaglutide?

Once weekly SubQ injection — abdomen, upper thigh, or upper arm. Rotate sites weekly. Use the shortest available needle (4–5mm). Inject at room temperature. Pinch 2 inches of skin, insert needle at 45–90 degrees, inject slowly, hold for 10 seconds before withdrawing. Dispose in sharps container.

Will semaglutide affect my hormones?

Significant weight loss — particularly reduction in visceral fat — typically improves testosterone in men and normalizes estrogen in both sexes. GLP-1 receptors are present in the ovaries and testes, and early research suggests direct hormonal modulation beyond weight effects. Monitor total/free testosterone and estradiol before and after 90 days of use.
